## Video 1: "7 Things You're Doing Right in Worldbuilding" (Wrestling With Words)
**Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f11d8byf3ms

### Key Principles
1. **Intentionality** — Every worldbuilding element must have a *purpose*. Don't include magical races, tech, or time periods just because they're genre defaults. Ask: *why does this exist in THIS story?*
2. **Core / Peripheral / Indulgent** — A hierarchy for worldbuilding elements:
   - **Core**: 3 pillars max that get deep exploration (more = textbook, fewer = shallow)
   - **Peripheral**: Adds depth, not critical to understand the story
   - **Indulgent**: Doesn't help the story but builds immersion; may never appear on-page
3. **Depth over breadth** — "Wide as an ocean, deep as a puddle" is the failure mode. Focus on fewer things explored from social, physical, and cultural angles
4. **POV as vehicle** — Different characters explore different worldbuilding elements. A diplomat sees culture/language; a soldier sees warfare/logistics. The world is *filtered through who's looking*
5. **Worldbuilding should expand the writer** — If researching your world teaches YOU something new, you're doing it right
6. **Consistency** — Internal rules that the audience can learn and predict. When people see a pattern and it pays off, they feel like the world is real
7. **Restraint** — The best worldbuilding leaves things unsaid. Mystery and implication > exposition dumps

## Video 2: "Modern Game Developers Forgot How to Make Characters" (Slight Cough)
**Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PcsyqZMItpo

### Key Principles (via TF2 Developer Commentary)
1. **Silhouette-first character design** — You should be able to identify a character from their silhouette alone, at any distance, in any lighting. This is THE priority
2. **Read hierarchy**: Team → Class → Weapon. Design communicates information in order of gameplay importance
3. **Color palette for faction** — Warm = Red team, Cool = Blue team. Instant team recognition
4. **Visual storytelling through equipment** — What a character carries tells you who they are. Scout's cleats = fast. Heavy's minigun = slow and dangerous. Engineer's hardhat = builder
5. **Weapons as personality** — TF2 designed primary weapons from 60s industrial product design, secondary/melee from musical instruments. Weapons reinforce character personality
6. **Contrast draws the eye** — Highest contrast areas are at chest level where weapons are held. Gradient from dark feet to bright chest pulls attention to the weapon
7. **Freedom within archetypes** — Heavy CAN play offense. Scout CAN defend. Classes suggest a role but don't enforce it. Player skill expression > rigid roles
8. **High skill ceiling** — Let individual mastery shine. A god-tier Spy terrorizing a 64-player server is a *feature*, not a balance problem. Investment should matter
9. **Characters you can HEAR** — Heavy's Russian accent, Scout's Brooklyn, Demoman's Scottish. Personality so strong you can hear them in your head years later

### Anti-patterns
- Characters with no personality, interchangeable designs ("Concord problem")
- Rigid role-locking that kills creativity (contrast with TF2's freedom)
- Prioritizing "everyone gets a medal" over skill expression
- Developers who don't play games making games

## Video 3: "How to Write a Tragic Villain" (Hello Future Me)
**Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vWYIEBlZ0fE

### Key Principles
1. **Inevitability** — The best tragic villains feel *inevitable*. The reader/player sees it coming but can't stop it. Dramatic irony: WE know catastrophe is coming; the characters don't
2. **Want vs Need inversion** — Normal arcs: character wants X but needs Y. Tragic villains KNOW what they need (love, acceptance, belonging) but are *denied* it until they snap into a new want (vengeance)
3. **Breaking point** — Show what's holding the villain together, then tear it away. The greater their effort to NOT become the villain, the greater the tragedy when they do
4. **The deeper villain** — Tragic villains reveal a *systemic* evil. Carrie's rampage indicts the bullies. Azula's breakdown indicts Ozai's abuse. The character is the symptom, not the disease
5. **Created, not born** — The most compelling tragic villains were shaped by forces outside their control: abuse, cults, programming, social structures. Fighting their own nature = fighting what someone else made them
6. **Mirror the hero** — Hero and villain share similar backstories. The hero's choices gain weight when we see what they COULD have become. The villain is a warning
7. **Redemption denied** — The cruelest tragedy: we glimpse humanity in the villain, see they COULD change... then the story kills them before they get the chance. "What almost was"
8. **Point of no return** — "If the world only sees me as the villain, then that's what I'll be." Villainy as catharsis, as reclaimed agency
9. **Perspective and timing of backstory** — WHEN we learn the villain's tragedy fundamentally changes our relationship to their actions. Reveal it too early = pity from the start. Too late = shock recontextualization

### Examples Referenced
- **Carrie** (Stephen King) — Bullied girl with telekinesis, prom blood scene as breaking point
- **Azula** (Avatar) — Raised as Ozai's weapon, rewarded only for cruelty, breakdown = indictment of her father
- **Puppet Master** (Ghost in the Shell) — Government AI denied personhood, product of surveillance state
- **Silent Hill franchise** — Claudia (cult abuse), Maria (created as literal manifestation of guilt)
- **Cyberpunk genre** — Tragic villains as commentary on systemic oppression
