# Design Philosophy — Synthesized from Research

*Principles drawn from TF2 developer commentary, worldbuilding theory, and tragic villain writing — applied to Genesis.*

---

## 1. CHARACTER DESIGN: Silhouette Is King

**From TF2:** You identify a character by silhouette first, color second, detail third.

**For Genesis:**
- **Adam**: Lean, upright, utility belt breaks the waist. Visor glow = instant recognition even in darkness
- **Eve (AI companion)**: Needs a fundamentally different shape — floaty, geometric, non-humanoid? Her silhouette should read "not human" instantly
- **Mecha Dragon**: Massive, angular, oppressive. Should fill the screen differently than anything else
- Every enemy type needs a unique silhouette. Crawlers = low/wide. Hoppers = compact/springy. Thornbacks = spiky/hunched
- **Test:** Black out every sprite. Can you still tell who's who? If not, redesign

## 2. WORLDBUILDING: Core/Peripheral/Indulgent

**The 3-Core Rule:** Only 3 worldbuilding pillars get deep exploration. Ours:

| Layer | Genesis Elements |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Core** | 1. The Fall / corruption of Eden (ecological collapse as spiritual metaphor) · 2. Adam & Eve's relationship (trust, dependence, divergence) · 3. What the Mecha Dragon actually IS (the mystery) |
| **Peripheral** | Federation politics, other survivors, technology lore, creature ecology |
| **Indulgent** | Pre-Fall civilization details, alien planet geology, deep tech specs |

**Intentionality check:** Every area, enemy, item, and NPC must serve at least one Core element. If it only serves Peripheral or Indulgent, it's optional content or gets cut.

## 3. THE MECHA DRAGON AS TRAGIC VILLAIN

This is where all three videos converge. The Mecha Dragon shouldn't be a generic big bad.

**Apply the tragic villain framework:**
- **Inevitability**: The player should feel the Dragon's arc building. Dramatic irony — we learn things about the Dragon's nature that Adam doesn't know yet
- **Want vs Need inversion**: The Dragon KNOWS what it needs (freedom? purpose? to protect something?) but is denied it. Its destructive actions are the result of being pushed past a breaking point
- **The deeper villain**: The Dragon should reveal a SYSTEMIC evil. Maybe the Federation created it. Maybe Eden's "paradise" was built on something terrible. The Dragon is the symptom, not the disease
- **Created, not born**: If the Dragon was engineered/programmed for a purpose it didn't choose, fighting its own nature IS the tragedy
- **Mirror Adam**: Adam and the Dragon should share something fundamental. Both displaced, both denied choice, both shaped by forces they didn't consent to. Adam's journey gains weight when we see the Dragon as what he COULD become
- **Breaking point**: Show us what was holding the Dragon back from full destruction. Then show us the moment it was taken away
- **Redemption denied?**: The cruelest version — Adam realizes too late that the Dragon could have been saved. Or: the Dragon sacrifices itself to undo the damage, but it's not enough

**Backstory reveal timing matters.** Don't explain the Dragon early. Let the player hate it, fear it, fight it. THEN reveal the tragedy. Recontextualize every encounter retroactively.

## 4. MYSTERY AS DESIGN PRINCIPLE

**From worldbuilding video:** The best worlds leave things unsaid. Restraint > exposition.

**For Genesis:**
- No lore dumps. Ever. The player pieces it together from environment, items, NPC fragments
- The world should feel like it existed before Adam arrived and will exist after
- Questions are more valuable than answers. For every answer we give, introduce a new question
- "Indulgent" worldbuilding can exist as discoverable flavor text — datapads, environmental storytelling — but never interrupts gameplay

## 5. FREEDOM WITHIN ARCHETYPES (Combat Design)

**From TF2:** Classes suggest roles but don't enforce them. Player mastery > rigid systems.

**For Genesis combat:**
- Weapons suggest playstyles but don't lock them. Fists are fast/close but a skilled player can use them aggressively at range via gap-closing (vault kicks, dashes)
- The stick's conditional hit-3 is exactly this — it rewards mastery without punishing beginners
- Every weapon should have a skill ceiling that separates good from great. Easy to use, hard to master
- Let players discover emergent combos (slide→vault kick→uppercut→air attack chains)
- **High skill ceiling is a feature.** A player who masters the whip should feel godlike, not normalized

## 6. VISUAL HIERARCHY IN GAMEPLAY

**From TF2:** Contrast draws the eye to what matters.

**For Genesis:**
- Player character should be the highest-contrast element on screen
- Enemy attacks/projectiles need to read instantly against any background
- Pickups/interactables need consistent visual language (glow, pulse, color)
- Environmental hazards vs safe ground should be readable at a glance
- Adam's cracked cyan visor = natural eye anchor point (bright against dark suit)

## 7. POV AS WORLDBUILDING VEHICLE

**From worldbuilding video:** Different characters see different aspects of the world.

**For Genesis:**
- Adam sees the world as a survivor — resources, threats, shelter
- Eve sees it as data — patterns, anomalies, connections Adam misses
- NPCs each have their own lens — a botanist sees the corrupted flora differently than a soldier
- The PLAYER's perspective shifts as they gain knowledge. Early game: "this is hostile territory." Late game: "this was someone's home"

---

*These aren't rules to follow rigidly. They're lenses to evaluate every design decision through. When in doubt: Does this serve a Core pillar? Does it have a unique silhouette? Does it deepen the mystery? Does it reward mastery?*
