# PROGRESSION MAP — Genesis

*Start-to-finish story spine. Every system, every mechanic, every area exists to serve this chain.*

---

## NARRATIVE FRAMEWORK

### The Genesis Parallel
| Scripture | Game |
|-----------|------|
| Adam as exalted spirit in heaven | Adam as high-ranking spacefarer returning from war |
| Satan's rebellion, cast out | Mecha Dragon's cosmic mission, attacks without warning |
| The Fall from heaven to earth | Crash landing on alien planet |
| Garden of Eden / creation of Adam's mortal identity | Recovery on-planet, identity forged through survival |
| Eve as companion, helpmate | EVE as AI companion whose relationship shifts post-crash |
| Temptation by the serpent | Dragon's terrifying, deceitful dialogue as Adam grows |
| Exile from the garden | Loss of technology, forced to adapt to hostile world |
| Building civilization (Cain, Abel, cities) | Settlement building, crafting, NPC communities |
| Redemption / return to God's presence | Final confrontation, choice, transcendence |

### The Central Mystery (withheld from player until late game)
**Why did the Dragon attack Adam specifically?**
- Not random. Not war. Something cosmic and personal.
- The Dragon is an instrument of causality — it HAD to happen. The tragedy is that the Dragon knows this and Adam doesn't.
- The Dragon's "cosmic mission" parallels Satan's role in LDS theology: opposition is necessary for growth. Without the Fall, there is no mortality, no agency, no becoming.
- **The twist:** The Dragon didn't attack Adam out of malice. It attacked because Adam *needed* to fall. The Dragon is the most tragic figure in the story because it destroys the thing it was made to serve.

### EVE's Arc
- **Pre-crash:** Ship's AI. Professional, capable, slightly cold. Thinks in data.
- **Post-crash:** Stranded with Adam. Still professional, but circumstances force intimacy.
- **Mid-game:** Develops opinions, personality, wit. Becomes combative when she disagrees. Starts caring about things beyond mission parameters.
- **Late game:** The "temptation" — EVE discovers something about the planet/Dragon/Adam's mission that Adam doesn't know. She has to decide whether to tell him (knowledge = the fruit). Her choice defines one of the endings.
- **The parallel:** Eve offering the fruit = EVE offering forbidden knowledge. But in this version, the knowledge might be *necessary* for Adam to transcend.

## ACT STRUCTURE

### PROLOGUE — "Heaven" (1 stage, ~10 min)
*SOTN-style opening. You play as powerful Adam.*

**Story:** Adam is aboard his ship, returning from [war/mission]. He's powerful — full abilities, advanced weapons, ship systems. EVE is the ship's AI, guiding him through routine operations. Something feels wrong. Sensors detect an anomaly. Then the Dragon appears — massive, silent, incomprehensible. It doesn't speak. It just *attacks*.

**Gameplay:**
- Tutorial disguised as competence. Player has full moveset temporarily
- Ship interior level — corridors, automated defenses failing
- You fight TO the escape pod / bridge, not against the Dragon directly
- The Dragon is visible in the background through windows — terrifyingly large
- You can't win. The ship is destroyed. Hard cut to black.

**Systems introduced:** Basic movement, combat (at full power), EVE as voice-in-ear
**Mystery established:** What IS that thing? Why did it attack? (GMTK: narrative enigma)
**Lock shown:** The Dragon. You can't fight it. Yet.

**Design notes:**
- Player feels powerful → then helpless. Contrast is the hook
- Dragon has NO dialogue. Just destruction. (Thomas Brush: mystery box in first 10 min)
- Don't over-explain. Ship alarms, EVE's clinical damage reports, then silence
- This stage is replayable later with full context — everything reads differently

### ACT 1 — "The Fall" (3-4 areas, ~2-3 hours)
*Crash landing. Survival. Discovery. Identity forged.*

#### 1A: The Wreckage (~30 min)
**Story:** Adam wakes in the ship's medical bay. Days/weeks have passed (EVE kept him alive). The ship is destroyed — most systems offline. Technology is already degrading (the planet corrodes advanced tech). EVE is now running on a portable device — reduced capabilities, same personality but shaken. She's never been outside a ship before.

Outside: alien wilderness. Strange flora, unfamiliar sounds. And in the distance — smoke. Someone else is here.

**Gameplay:**
- Stripped to basics: no weapons, no abilities. You have fists and EVE
- Scavenge the ship for salvageable parts (crafting tutorial — natural, not forced)
- EVE scans wreckage: "This alloy is degrading. The atmosphere is... reactive"
- First enemy encounters: wildlife. Crawlers, small swarms. Not hostile unless provoked
- **Funsie:** Smashable ship debris, sparking consoles you can punch

**Systems introduced:**
- EVE scanning (knowledge keys begin)
- Basic crafting (axe from ship debris — dual use: melee + resource gathering)
- Technology degradation concept (your ship gear breaks, forcing adaptation)
- Swarm behavior: "EVE: They likely won't bother you if you walk slowly" (teach through EVE, not overlay)

**Mystery:** What happened while you were unconscious? (EVE is evasive about something)
**Lock:** Dense forest/cliff blocks southern path. Need better tools.

#### 1B: First Contact (~30 min)
**Story:** Follow the smoke. Find a small native settlement — they've been watching you. They found your crash site. Maybe they've been tending to you (the settlement grew around the ship while Adam recovered). Communication is impossible — their language is alien. But they're not hostile. EVE begins analyzing their speech patterns.

One native stands out — seems to be a leader/elder. They gesture toward the mountains. Something up there concerns them.

**Gameplay:**
- Meet first NPCs. Can't communicate yet — gesture-based interaction
- EVE: "I'm picking up linguistic patterns. Give me time" (translation progression starts)
- Settlement acts as hub: rest point, crafting station, storage
- Natives give you a crude weapon (stick/spear) through gesture — your first real melee weapon
- First fetch task, but SOTN-style: the native points at something in the wilderness. Going there is exploring anyway. You find something cool AND what they wanted.

**Systems introduced:**
- NPC interaction (pre-translation)
- Settlement as hub
- Weapon acquisition (found/gifted, not crafted yet)
- EVE translation progress (background system, not grindable)

**Mystery:** What are the natives afraid of in the mountains?
**Lock:** Mountain path blocked by a creature (first mini-boss territory)

#### 1C: The Wilds (~45 min)
**Story:** Explore the region around the settlement. Multiple paths — forest, river, caves. Each has its own ecology, enemies, and scannable discoveries. EVE becomes more animated as she catalogs the planet. "This is remarkable. The soil composition suggests..." She's a scientist who's never had a real lab before.

The planet fights back. Weather system — getting soaked makes you cold. EVE suggests wax coating (paraffin wax from certain plants + linseed-equivalent oil). You craft your first protective gear.

**Gameplay:**
- Open exploration area. Multiple biomes accessible
- Bow and arrows craftable (EVE identifies flexible wood + sinew equivalent)
- Environmental hazards: rain/cold, toxic spores, unstable terrain
- Scannable invisible tiles (slight glint). EVE reveals hidden resources, lore fragments
- Enemy variety: Crawlers patrol, Hoppers guard territory, Thornbacks block paths
- **First enemy corpse scanning:** EVE examines a defeated creature, reveals weakness of next type

**Systems introduced:**
- Weather/environmental effects
- Ranged weapon crafting
- Invisible/scannable tiles (Metroidbrainia: knowledge unlocks)
- Bestiary building through scanning
- Environmental crafting (shelter, waterproofing)

**Mystery:** Ancient ruins in the caves. Not native-built. Something was here before. (Moldy worldbuilding: ruins atop ruins)
**Lock:** The mountain pass. Something big patrols it.

#### 1D: The Guardian — FIRST BOSS (~15 min)
**Story:** The thing the natives fear. A massive corrupted creature guarding the mountain pass. Not evil — territorial. EVE scans it mid-fight: "It's protecting something. There's a structure behind it."

**Gameplay:**
- First real boss. Tests all Act 1 mechanics: melee, ranged, dodge, environmental awareness
- Multi-phase (per our design principles): Phase 1 = ground fight, Phase 2 = it climbs the walls (vertical platforming), Phase 3 = it's desperate, faster, but EVE calls out a weakness she scanned in Phase 1
- Defeating it opens the mountain pass
- Behind it: ancient ruins. Pre-native. Pre-everything. And carved into the wall — an image that looks like a dragon

**Systems tested:** Everything from Act 1. If the player can beat this, they understand the game.
**Mystery deepened:** Who built these ruins? Why is the Dragon carved here? How old IS this?
**Reward:** Access to Act 2 + a crafted weapon from the Guardian's remains (EVE: "The chitin is incredibly dense. We could...")

### ACT 2 — "Exile" (4-5 areas, ~3-4 hours)
*Expansion. Technology loss deepens. EVE grows. The Dragon returns.*

#### 2A: The Ancient Ruins
**Story:** Explore the structure the Guardian was protecting. It's a temple/facility from a civilization that predates the natives by millennia. EVE is overwhelmed with data — inscriptions, technology fragments, biological samples. 

Key discovery: this civilization KNEW about the Dragon. It's depicted throughout — not as a destroyer, but as... something else. A force of nature. A necessary thing.

**Gameplay:**
- Puzzle-heavy area. Knowledge keys (inscriptions EVE translates partially)
- New traversal challenges: crumbling platforms, timed sections, dark areas
- Scannable lore everywhere — heavy optional depth
- New enemy type: automated guardians of the ruins (still functioning after millennia)

**EVE development:** She becomes obsessed with the ruins. First real disagreement with Adam — she wants to stay and study, he needs to keep moving. "You don't understand what this MEANS."

**Mystery:** The ancients didn't fight the Dragon. They... accepted it? Worshipped it? Served it?

#### 2B: The Frontier
**Story:** Beyond the mountains. A larger world opens up. More settlements, more natives, more danger. Technology from the ship is almost entirely degraded now — Adam relies on crafted gear, native techniques, and scavenged ancient tech.

The natives here are different from the first settlement. Different culture, different relationship with the land. Some are hostile. EVE's translation is improving — fragments of conversation emerge.

**Gameplay:**
- World opens significantly. Multiple paths and optional areas
- Gun crafting becomes possible (hybrid: ancient tech + native materials)
- Settlement building expands: you can establish outposts (sim mode lite)
- More weapon variety — melee weapons found in the wild that break (durability system)
- Special weapon moves unlock (sling air slam, gun radial spray)

**Systems introduced:**
- Gun/ranged progression
- Settlement building / sim mode
- Weapon durability
- Special moves per weapon
- EVE delegation (she can talk to natives for you now, summarizes conversations)

#### 2C: The Signal
**Story:** EVE detects something. A signal — technological, not natural. Coming from deep in hostile territory. It's not from Adam's ship. It's not ancient. It's *current*.

This is the mid-game pivot. Someone or something else is here. And it has technology.

**Gameplay:**
- Journey toward the signal through increasingly dangerous territory
- New biome: corrupted land. Something is wrong here — flora is dying, fauna is aggressive and mutated
- The corruption has a source. It's spreading.
- Environmental storytelling: abandoned native settlements, signs of flight

**EVE development:** She's picking up something in the signal. It's... familiar. She won't say why. (She's hiding something.)

**Mystery:** What is the signal? Why does EVE recognize it? What caused the corruption?

#### 2D: The Dragon Returns — SECOND MAJOR ENCOUNTER
**Story:** You find the signal source: wreckage. Not your ship — another one. Older. And inside... evidence that someone else crashed here. Long ago. They didn't survive.

And then the Dragon appears. For the first time since the prologue. 

But this time it's different. This time, it *speaks*.

Its voice is wrong — multiple tones, like something pretending to be one voice. And what it says is terrifying because it's not a threat. It's an offer. Or a warning. Or a prophecy. The player can't quite tell.

"You are not the first. You will not be the last. But you might be the one who understands."

Then it leaves.

**Gameplay:**
- NOT a boss fight. The Dragon appears, the screen shakes, enemies flee
- It speaks one cryptic line and vanishes
- You explore the other crash site — find advanced but corroded technology
- EVE is deeply shaken. She knows something. She won't say what.
- **Surprise/twist:** Among the wreckage, you find a data core. EVE can read it. What's on it changes things.

**Mystery ERUPTS:**
- Who else crashed here?
- Why did the Dragon say "you might be the one who understands"?
- What does EVE know?
- What's on the data core?

### ACT 3 — "Knowledge" (3-4 areas, ~3-4 hours)
*Truth emerges. EVE's temptation. The fruit.*

#### 3A: The Data Core
**Story:** EVE processes the data core. It takes time (gameplay continues while she works in background). Over the next several areas, she feeds you fragments:

- The other crash was deliberate. Someone was SENT here.
- This planet isn't random. It's... significant. A nexus. A test.
- The Dragon has been here since before the ancients. It predates everything on this world.
- And: there are references to more crashers. Many more. Over millennia. None survived long-term.

Adam is not special. He's the latest in a long line. Unless he does something different.

**Gameplay:**
- This revelation unfolds DURING normal exploration/combat, not cutscenes
- EVE delivers fragments as you explore new areas, fight new enemies
- The world gets harder — corruption is spreading faster
- Native settlements need defending (sim mode becomes urgent, not optional)
- Body armor becomes craftable. Advanced melee weapons. You're becoming a hybrid warrior.

#### 3B: EVE's Discovery (The Fruit)
**Story:** EVE finishes processing the data core. She's found something she didn't expect. Something about Adam. About why he was specifically targeted by the Dragon.

This is the temptation scene. EVE has knowledge that would change everything — but sharing it might break Adam. Or save him. She doesn't know which.

She confronts Adam: "I found something. About you. About why we're here. Do you want to know?"

**The player chooses:** Yes or Not Yet.

If Yes: EVE reveals [the truth — see Endgame section]
If Not Yet: The knowledge haunts the rest of the game. EVE acts differently. She's carrying a burden.

**Either way, the game continues.** But the player's understanding of everything shifts.

#### 3C: The Dragon's Lair Approach
**Story:** Armed with (or deliberately ignorant of) EVE's knowledge, Adam pushes toward the Dragon's territory. The corruption is thickest here. This is where it all started.

The natives have legends about this place. EVE can fully translate now. Their stories align with the ancient ruins — the Dragon is not an invader. It lives here. It's PART of this world. Maybe the most important part.

**Gameplay:**
- Hardest areas in the game. All mechanics tested
- Optional: return to settlements to prepare, upgrade, say goodbye to NPCs
- The landscape itself is hostile — alien, beautiful, terrifying
- Ancient tech and native tech fuse in the most powerful gear
- EVE is at full capability — scanning reveals deep secrets, crafting reaches peak

---

### ACT 4 — "Confrontation" (2 areas + final boss, ~1-2 hours)

#### 4A: The Dragon's Domain
**Story:** The heart of the corruption. Except... it's not corrupted. It's *transformed*. The land here is alien but alive. Not dying — becoming something else. The Dragon hasn't been destroying the world. It's been *changing* it.

EVE: "I don't think it's corruption. I think it's... evolution. Forced evolution."

The Dragon is an agent of change. Causality incarnate. It breaks things so they can become something new. That's its cosmic mission. That's its tragedy — it can never stop. It destroys because it must, so that growth can follow.

#### 4B: Final Boss — The Mecha Dragon
**Story:** The confrontation. The Dragon speaks freely now. It's intelligent, articulate, and deeply sad. It doesn't want to fight Adam. But it will, because that's what it was made to do.

"I was made to break what cannot grow. You were made to grow from what is broken. We are the same machine."

**Gameplay:**
- Multi-phase boss fight. The best fight in the game
- Phase 1: The Dragon attacks with full force. Pure combat test
- Phase 2: The arena transforms. Platforming + combat. The Dragon uses the environment
- Phase 3: The Dragon hesitates. Its attacks have openings that weren't there before. It's *letting* you win — or testing if you've grown enough
- Phase 4 (if player has full knowledge from EVE): The Dragon reveals its true form. Not a monster — a mechanism. Beautiful and terrible. The fight becomes something else entirely

**The Dragon's defeat is not triumphant.** It's melancholy. The Dragon falls, and its last words recontextualize the entire game.

### ENDINGS

#### Ending A: "Link the Fire" (Standard)
Adam defeats the Dragon. The corruption recedes. The planet heals. Adam stays, builds a new civilization with the natives. EVE remains his companion. Bittersweet — he can never go home, but he's found purpose.

*This ending is satisfying but incomplete. The player feels something is missing.*

#### Ending B: "The Dark Lord" (Hidden, requires full knowledge + specific actions)
Adam discovers that the Dragon's death doesn't end the cycle — another will come. Another Adam will crash. Another Dragon will attack. Unless someone takes the Dragon's place.

Adam can CHOOSE to become the new agent of causality. He takes the Dragon's role — not destroying, but ensuring that growth continues. He leaves the planet. EVE stays behind to guide the next one.

This ending is hidden behind: collecting all ancient lore fragments (scannable tiles), getting EVE's full revelation, and making a specific choice in the final confrontation.

*This ending is the "true" ending. It recontextualizes Adam from survivor to cosmic figure. It parallels the LDS concept of exaltation — becoming something greater through suffering.*

#### Ending C: "The Third Path" (Ultra-hidden, community puzzle?)
Something even the developers barely hint at. Knowledge-locked. Maybe never fully solved.

*GMTK principle: the game should always be just a little bit unknowable.*

## OPEN DESIGN QUESTIONS (2026-03-20)

### 1. The Cycle / EVE's Discovery
- Maybe the cycle exists to preserve life on planets throughout the system
- The Dragon as a mechanism for seeding/forcing growth on worlds — crashes are deliberate cultivation
- Still exploring what's believable. The revelation needs to feel earned, not contrived
- Key tension: what truth could EVE find that would genuinely shake Adam (and the player)?

### 2. Ending B — Taking the Dragon's Place
- Ethan notes it feels Dragon's Dogma-esque (that game literally has this ending)
- Core question: HOW and WHY would Adam become the Dragon? What transformation makes that plausible?
- Fun idea, but needs mechanical/narrative justification beyond "someone has to do it"
- Might need a different hidden ending that's more unique to Genesis's identity
- Don't discard yet — iterate on it

### 3. The Natives
- Intentionally vague. Mystery preserved.
- They have their own creation myth explaining their existence — possibly conflicting accounts between settlements
- Maybe we're NOT meant to know their true origin. Players theory-craft
- This aligns with principle: "the game should always be a little bit unknowable"
- Different native groups might have contradictory origin stories — all plausible, none confirmed

### 4. Dragon's Dialogue Arc
- **Early (Prologue):** Silent. Pure destruction. No communication
- **Mid (Act 2D, first return):** Speaks, but terrifying and cryptic. Multiple tones. Feels alien and deceitful. The player doesn't trust anything it says
- **Late (Act 3-4):** Increasingly articulate and melancholy as Adam grows "spiritually." The terror recedes and tragedy emerges. The player starts to pity it
- This mirrors Adam's growth — the Dragon doesn't change, Adam's ability to understand it does

### 5. Story Beat Chain (Next Step)
- Need ~20-30 sequential story beats from inciting incident to conclusion
- Not a book — a chain of events, each one a sentence or two
- Each beat ties to a gameplay milestone
- Leaves interesting ambiguity (good stories don't answer everything)
- Goal: development never stalls because the next beat always tells you what to build next
